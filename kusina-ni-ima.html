5. How does the ideal mechanicaladvantage (IMA) of an inclined plane vary with the angle of the plane; that is, whattrigonometric function of a gives the value of IMA?​


Answer:

The mechanical advantage is a measure of the usefulness of a machine and its value is given by the ratio of the force that "goes out" from the machine (the output force) to the force that "goes in" the machine (the input force). We can also define it as the measure of the amplified force that was gained by the system.

Note: A simple machine (in our case an incline) does not reduce the amount of work (that would be against the conservation of energy principle), only the force needed to be applied is smaller. But the distance is longer !

The mechanical advantage measured as defined above is also called Actual Mechanical Advantage or AMA.

and, as a ratio of forces, it is unitless. In general, AMA takes into account the friction force as well as all the other forces acting on the object so we must interpret the input force as a resultant of all these forces

For an inclined plane, we can consider or neglect the friction (theoretically). In the case of a frictionless incline, we define the Ideal Mechanical Advantage (IMA) as the ratio of the input distance to the output distance. It is also unitless but it is easier to measure.

So, to summarize, for an incline the output force is the weight, G, of the object and the input force is the force, parallel with the incline, that we need to apply to raise the object from the starting point (usually the base of the incline) to a required height, h, along the surface, of the incline of length L.
