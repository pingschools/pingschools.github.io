It's superego

The three components of the human personality that makeup Sigmund Freud's psychoanalytic personality theory are known by the labels id, ego, and superego. These three components, in accordance with Freud, work together to produce the complicated behavior of people.

Id: Meeting Basic Needs

The most fundamental component of the psyche, the id, seeks immediate satisfaction for our needs and wants. A person gets tense or uncomfortable if certain requirements or wants are not addressed.

Sally needed to drink. She stretched across the table and drank from Mr. Smith's water glass, much to his astonishment, instead of waiting for the service to refill her glass.A crying infant demanded food till satisfied.A young child cried nonstop until she was given another serving of dessert because she wanted more.

Ego: Dealing with Reality

In order to satisfy the needs d's in a way that is accepted by society, the ego deals with reality. Delaying gratification may assist relieve the tension the id experiences when a desire is not immediately satiated. The ego understands that being selfish isn't always in our best interests in the long term and that other people have needs and wants as well.

Sally needed to drink. She really simply wanted to drink from Mr. Smith's glass, but she knew her server would be back soon to refill her water glass, so she waited until then to grab a drink.Michael needed money, but he chose against robbing the cash register since he didn't want to get caught.Amy desperately wanted to stuff a handful of croutons into her mouth while she was waiting in line at the salad bar. She chose to delay sitting down to eat for a few seconds though because her supervisor was present.

Superego: Adding Morals

The superego, which is founded on morality and judgments about good and evil, emerges last. Even while the superego and the ego may come to the same conclusion about something, the superego bases its choice more on moral principles than the ego does, which bases its choice more on what others will think or the potential outcomes of a course of action.

Sarah was aware that she could take the materials from the office without anyone noticing. She would probably never get caught, but she opted not to steal anything because she knew it was wrong.Despite studying, Maggie was unable to recall the response to exam question #12. Nate was the brightest student in the class, and if Maggie turned her head just a little bit, she could read his responses. Maggie was on the verge of cheating when Mrs. Archer turned around, but she was restrained by her morality because she knew it was wrong. Instead, Maggie made an educated guess and submitted her assignment.Tom had many chances to cheat on his wife when he was gone on business. He made the decision to stay away from the women who had shown interest in him, though, because he was aware of the negative effects such behavior would have on his family.

6. What is The meaning of Feud?​


Explanation

A feud is a long-standing and bitter conflict between two or more parties or individuals.

A feud is a long-standing and bitter conflict or disagreement between two individuals, families, or groups. It typically involves strong negative emotions such as anger, resentment, and hostility, and may be based on real or perceived grievances, disputes, or differences in beliefs, values, or interests. Feuds can be fueled by a variety of factors such as pride, revenge, jealousy, or competition, and may escalate over time, leading to further animosity and even violence. In some cases, feuds may be resolved through negotiation, mediation, or other means of conflict resolution, while in others they may persist for generations, causing ongoing harm and division.
