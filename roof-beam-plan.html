9. how to develop roof framing plan?​


Answer:

Roofs are framed in five basic designs: shed, gable, hip, gambrel and mansard. The gable is the most common, and it can be complicated with multiple roof lines, including valleys and dormers.

In order to build any but a shed-type roof, including trusses, you’ll need to first determine a few factors; the span, rise, run and line length. For a shed roof you only need to know the rise, span and line length. Span is the measurement across the building from outside supporting wall to outside supporting wall. Run is half the distance of the span. Rise is the measurement from the centerline of the span to the top of the roof line. Line length is the measurement from the outside of the supporting wall line to the centerline of the roof at the top of the rise. Basically you’re working with a triangle with two legs and the line length for a hypotenuse.

Naturally, he wants to place the roof beams in such an angle that each pair of opposite beams would meet exactly in the middle. What is the angle of elevation, in degrees, of the roof beams? Round your final answer to the nearest tenth. ^\circ ∘ degrees


Answer:

28.1°

Step-by-step explanation:

The roof will be in the shape of an isosceles triangle with a base length of 30 m and two sides that are 17 m. The two 17 m beams will have the same angle of elevation since they have to might in the middle.

So to find the angle of elevation, we can split the roof in half vertically to create a right triangle. The base will now be 15 m, and the hypotenuse will be 17. Now we can use a trigonometry function to solve for the angle. We know the hypotenuse and the side adjacent to the angle, so we can use cosine.

[tex] \cosθ = \frac{adjacent}{hypotenuse} \\ \\ \cosθ = \frac{15}{17} \\ \\ \cos^{ - 1} ( \frac{15}{17} ) = θ \: \\ \\ θ = 28.1°[/tex]

27. Identify the parts of a roof plan.​


PARTS OF ROOF PLAN

Answer:

The following are the components of a house's roof:

Roof plane: This is the roof's surface. It's flat, yet it's pitched or angled. It's also known as the roof's field.

Ridge: The top or peak of the roof, where two roof planes meet, is known as the ridge.

Valley: Two pitched roof sides unite and project inward to form a valley. They're always on a lower incline than the roof planes around them.

Dormer: A dormer is a roof feature that extends beyond the roof face. It usually has a window and provides more space and/or light to the interior room.

Abutment: Instead of a roof ridge, an abutment is a location where the roof face meets the home's wall. Abutments are frequently used on modest home additions like entrance canopies, bay windows, attached outbuildings, and garages.

Hip: When two roof faces meet and protrude outward, this is called a hip.

Gable: The triangular wall beneath two roof planes is known as a gable. It's vital to remember that gables aren't found on every roof.

Hipped end: The sloped roof face between two roof hips is known as the hipped end of the roof. The four hipped ends of a pyramid roof. There are only two hipped ends to a hipped roof.

Gable end: The gable end of a roof is the edge of a roof that extends over the gable.

Eave: The border of the outer wall where the roof hangs over.
