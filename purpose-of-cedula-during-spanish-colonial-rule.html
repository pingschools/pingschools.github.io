1. Purpose of cedula during Spanish colonial rule TASKS: 1. Research and compare briefly the use of cedula during the Spanish colonial rule in the Philippines to its current purpose. Today’s purpose of cedula​


Answer:

The cedula is a reminder of Spain's tyranny against Filipinos who were branded as “indios” during the invaders' 300-year rule. It's a piece of paper that symbolizes the Spaniards' oppression and tearing the same means the start of our fight for freedom and independence. During the Spaniard rule, cedula is issued to Filipinos to pay their taxes to the Spanish government.

Nowadays, cedula is a one-fourth sheet of paper that acknowledges your membership or registration as a resident in a town or city. This is needed to perform a process such as acquiring a national ID or taking part in a legal process.

4. Life, beliefs and practices of Filipino during the Pre-colonial and Spanish Colonial period. ​


Answer:

The Philippines proudly boasts to be the only Christian nation in Asia. More than 86 percent of the population is Roman Catholic, 6 percent belong to various nationalized Christian cults, and another 2 percent belong to well over 100 Protestant denominations.

The nature of religion in the pre-colonial Philippines is often unclear. Religions present include animism, indigenous religious beliefs and mythologies such as Anito and influences from Hinduism and Buddhism. The earliest pieces of evidence that exist are archaeological finds including Hindu–Buddhist gold statues. The earliest written evidence comes from the Laguna Copperplate Inscription, dated to around 900 CE, which uses the Buddhist–Hindu lunar calendar. With the arrival of Islam in the 14th century, the older religions gradually disappeared, and after the arrival of Ferdinand Magellan in 1521 Christianity, specifically Roman Catholicism, became the dominant religion.

6. Which event was viewed by the Spanish government officials as a move to overturn the Spanish colonial rule in the Philippines?


This event is the 1872 CAVITE MUTINY.  This uprising took place in the province of Cavite, hence it is called Cavite Mutiny.  This unsuccessful mutiny was participated in by around 200 soldiers and laborers of the Engineering and Artillery Corps after subjecting them to taxes by Governor-general Rafael de Izquerdo, by which they were previously exempt.  The Spaniards notice this uprising to be dangerous and viewed it as an initial move to overturn their colonial rule.  Hence, they impose heavy burdens not just on taxes but to force labors and executions to any Filipinos deemed by them to be dangerous.  This led to the execution of three famous martyrs by garrote namely GOMBURZA, short for Mariano Gomez, Jose Burgos, and Jacinto Zamora.

18. Why are filipinos allowed to hold the position of gobernadorcillo during the spanish colonial rule?


Answer:

During the Spanish colonial period in the Philippines, gobernadorcillos (also known as capitan municipal) were local leaders who were appointed to manage the day-to-day affairs of a town or municipality. They were usually chosen from among the local elite, and were often wealthy landowners or prominent members of the community. In some cases, gobernadorcillos were also allowed to hold positions in the local government, such as serving as a member of the cabildo (municipal council).

Filipinos were allowed to hold the position of gobernadorcillo because they were considered part of the Spanish colonial system, and were expected to follow the laws and regulations set by the Spanish authorities. However, it is important to note that the gobernadorcillos were not elected by the people, but were instead appointed by the Spanish colonial government, and their powers and responsibilities were often limited. Despite this, the gobernadorcillo played an important role in the local community, and was responsible for enforcing laws, collecting taxes, and representing the interests of the people to the Spanish authorities.

What is the status of the Philippines during Spanish colonization?

The Philippines was ruled under the Mexico-based Viceroyalty of New Spain. After this, the colony was directly governed by Spain. Spanish rule ended in 1898 with Spain's defeat in the Spanish–American War. The Philippines then became a territory of the United States.

What are the reasons of Spanish colonization in the Philippines?

Spain had three objectives in its policy toward the Philippines, its only colony in Asia: to acquire a share in the spice trade, to develop contacts with China and Japan in order to further Christian missionary efforts there, and to convert the Filipinos to Christianity.
