1. A 100.0-g sample of hot water and a 10.0-g sample of aluminum pellets at room temperature are mixed in an insulated thermos bottle and allowed to equilibrate. If the initial temperature of the pellets is 25.0°C, and the initial temperature of the hot water is 90.0°C, what was the final temperature in °C? Specific Heat Capacity of Liquid Water = 4.184 J/g-°C Specific Heat Capacity of Aluminum = 0.903 J/g-°C Report your answer in correct number of significant figures.​


Answer:

To find the final temperature, we need to use the equation of heat transfer:

Q = mcΔT

where Q is the heat transferred, m is the mass, c is the specific heat capacity, and ΔT is the change in temperature.

Explanation:

We have two heat transfers happening here:

1. Heat lost by the hot water = m1c1ΔT1

2. Heat gained by the aluminum pellets = m2c2ΔT2

The heat lost by the hot water is equal to the heat gained by the aluminum pellets. So,

m1c1ΔT1 = -m2c2ΔT2

We know that the mass of hot water is 100.0 g and the specific heat capacity of liquid water is 4.184 J/g-°C.

The mass of aluminum pellets is 10.0 g and the specific heat capacity of aluminum is 0.903 J/g-°C.

Initial temperature of the pellets is 25.0°C and the initial temperature of the hot water is 90.0°C.

So,

(100.0g)(4.184 J/g-°C)(Tf - 90.0°C) = -(10.0g)(0.903 J/g-°C)(Tf - 25.0°C)

Solving for Tf, we get

Tf = (100.0g)(4.184 J/g-°C)(90.0°C) + (10.0g)(0.903 J/g-°C)(25.0°C) / (100.0g)(4.184 J/g-°C) + (10.0g)(0.903 J/g-°C)

Tf = (41840 + 225.75) / (418.4 + 0.903)

Tf = 42065.75 / 419.303

Tf = 99.9°C

Therefore, the final temperature of the mixture is 99.9°C.


2. A 330 mL sample of gas is initially at a pressure of 650 torr and a temperature of 32°C. If this gas is compressed to a volume of 242 mL and the pressure increases to 901 torr, what will be the new temperature of the gas (reported in °C)? [1 torr = 1 mm Hg]


SOLUTION:

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} P_1 & = \text{650 torr} \\ V_1 & = \text{330 mL} \\ T_1 & = 32^{\circ}\text{C} = \text{305 K} \\ P_2 & = \text{901 torr} \\ V_2 & = \text{242 mL} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the final temperature in kelvin by using combined gas law.

[tex]\begin{aligned} T_2 & = \frac{T_1P_2V_2}{P_1V_1} \\ & = \frac{(\text{305 K})(\text{901 torr})(\text{242 mL})}{(\text{650 torr})(\text{330 mL})} \\ & = \text{310 K} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Convert the final temperature from kelvin to degree Celsius.

[tex]\begin{aligned} t_2 & = T_2 - 273 \\ & = 310 - 273 \\ & = \boxed{37^{\circ}\text{C}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the new temperature of the gas is 37°C.

11. It was reported in a daily newspaper that the Filipino men in Metro manila aged 50 to 60 years, exercise an average of 14 hours a week. A sports’ columnist initial reaction, upon reading the article, is that the average figure is a bit too high. He decides to investigate by interviewing a small sample of 25 men in Metro Manila aged 50 to 60 years. He finds that the average number of exercise hour per week is 11 hours with a standard deviation of 2 hours. At 5% level, was the sports writer correct in thinking that the figure stated in the newspaper was high?


To determine whether the sports writer was correct in thinking that the figure stated in the newspaper was high, we can conduct a hypothesis test using the sample data.

Let's define our null and alternative hypotheses:

Null hypothesis (H0): The true population mean exercise hours per week (μ) is equal to or less than 14 hours (μ ≤ 14).
Alternative hypothesis (Ha): The true population mean exercise hours per week (μ) is greater than 14 hours (μ > 14).
We will use a one-sample t-test to test these hypotheses, with a significance level of 0.05 (or 5%).

The test statistic can be calculated as:

t = (x̄ - μ) / (s / √n)

where x̄ is the sample mean, μ is the hypothesized population mean, s is the sample standard deviation, and n is the sample size.

Substituting the values we have:

t = (11 - 14) / (2 / √25) = -7.5

The critical t-value for a one-tailed test with 24 degrees of freedom (n-1) and a significance level of 0.05 is 1.711.

Since our calculated t-value of -7.5 is less than the critical t-value of 1.711, we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that the true population mean exercise hours per week is significantly less than 14 hours at a 5% level of significance. Therefore, the sports writer was correct in thinking that the figure stated in the newspaper was high.
