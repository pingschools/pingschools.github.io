15. The penhold grip is executed by grasping the racket as if to shake hands with it. (table tennis)


The penhold grip is executed by grasping the racket as if to shake hands with it. (table tennis)

 

The second most common form of grip would have been the penhold grip. There are three main variations of  this type of grip, namely the standard Chinese grip, the Japanese or Korean grip, and the back grip.  One handles the paddle handle the same way one might hold the pen as one is about to write, with the thumb  and index finger in front of the handle, and the other three fingers curled tightly around the back of the support  racket.

There are other variations in this grip, where one can hold one's thumb almost parallel to the blade while the  index finger is curved. The length of the gap between the thumb and the index finger can vary. The index finger can overlap slightly with the thumb. The remaining fingers on the back will reach the edge, either all or some.  The location of the remaining fingers on the back that vary from the middle to the base of the blade.

The downside of this grip is that it proves to be a challenging task to execute consistent backhand topspins, as  this would allow the player to contort his arm to an unnatural degree. It is therefore less likely for the penhold  grip players to flexibly touch either side of the table, which requires the penhold style to strike and protect the  table with all forehand attacks. This would naturally exhaust the player's stamina quicker if, and only if, the  player has enough footwork to keep up with the ball in the first place.

Players that use this grip often tend to remain closer to the surface, move or block with a backhand, and strike with a forehand stroke, either with a drive or a topspin or a loop. This type of grip is the most common of all  three, preferred by the Chinese and often appearing on the international stage.

The Shakehand Grip

This form of grip is popular among Western players, but it has also spread across Asian and European  countries. The name is derived from the usual handshake as the player is going to take a racket similar to someone who's going to offer a handshake.  

The key styles of shakehand grip are the deep grip of the shakehand and the shallow grip of the shakehand.  

These two are subtly different from each other and require a keen eye to see the difference.

The biggest difference between the shallow and the deep form is where the thumb is located.  Your thumb will relax on the rubber for a deep grip, while your thumb will relax on the edge for a shallow grip.  Thumb positioning on the racket determines how precise and fast the ball can be returned.

Answer:1.) The place where table tennis originated.= England

○ The game was invented in England in the early days of the 20th century and was originally called Ping-Pong, a trade name. The name table tennis was adopted in 1921–22 when the old Ping-Pong Association formed in 1902 was revived.

2.) The organization who given table tennis.= The International Table Tennis Federation (ITTF)

○ The International Table Tennis Federation (ITTF) is the governing body for all national table tennis associations.

3.) Another name of table tennis.= Ping-Pong

○ Table tennis, also called (trademark) Ping-Pong, ball game similar in principle to lawn tennis and played on a flat table divided into two equal courts by a net fixed across its width at the middle.

4.) The first ball of table tennis.= Celluloid Ball

○ The first celluloid ball was introduced in England in 1900, at a time when table tennis had traditionally been more of a parlor game than a sport, using balls made from cork or other materials.
